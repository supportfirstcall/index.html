4. Hip fracture

Break in the upper femur near the hip joint. Common after falls in elderly patients. Often requires surgical repair. Post-op care includes PT, DVT prophylaxis, and pain management.

5. Skin and soft tissue infections (SSTIs)

Bacterial infections of the skin and underlying tissue. Common types: cellulitis, infected wounds, infected pressure injuries. Treated with antibiotics for a defined course (typically 5–14 days).

6. Wounds and pressure injuries

Pressure injuries form over bony prominences (sacrum, heels, hips). Staged I–IV by depth. Review wound care provider notes daily.

L89.151 — Sacral, stage 1L89.152 — Sacral, stage 2L89.153 — Sacral, stage 3L89.154 — Sacral, stage 4L89.150 — Sacral, unstageableL89.621 — Left heel, stage 1L89.622 — Left heel, stage 2
Always review wound care provider notes for: current stage, wound size (cm), wound bed description, drainage type, dressing type, and signs of infection. Flag any deteriorating wounds for physician.

9. Cognitive impairment / dementia

10. Hypothyroidism

Underactive thyroid gland. Very common in elderly women. Managed with daily levothyroxine. Monitored with TSH blood levels.

E03.9 — Hypothyroidism, unspecified (most common)E06.3 — Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's)E89.0 — Post-procedural hypothyroidism
MedicationClassNotes
Levothyroxine (Synthroid, Levoxyl)Thyroid hormone replacementTake on empty stomach; dose adjusted by TSH
Liothyronine (Cytomel)T3 thyroid hormoneLess common; combination therapy
Levothyroxine is linked to HYPOTHYROIDISM only. Do not document "fatigue" or "feeling cold" — document hypothyroidism (E03.9) instead.

11. COPD and asthma

COPD is a chronic lung condition from airflow obstruction, most often from smoking history. Asthma is reversible airway inflammation. Do not use COPD and asthma interchangeably — they are different diagnoses.

12. Heart failure (CHF)

The heart cannot pump blood effectively. Very common in post-acute care. Two main types: HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction) and HFpEF (preserved ejection fraction). Managed with diuretics, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, and beta blockers.

I50.9 — Heart failure, unspecified (use if type not documented)I50.30 — Unspecified diastolic heart failure (HFpEF)I50.20 — Unspecified systolic heart failure (HFrEF)I50.1 — Left ventricular failureI50.810 — Right heart failure

Lisinopril / EnalaprilACE inhibitorReduces cardiac workload
Carvedilol / Metoprolol succinateBeta blockerImproves cardiac function over time

Furosemide (Lasix) is most commonly linked to HEART FAILURE, not diabetes. Always confirm with physician.

13. Chronic kidney disease (CKD)

Progressive loss of kidney function. Staged 1–5 by GFR. Stage 5 is end-stage renal disease (ESRD). Very common as a complication of diabetes and hypertension. Significantly affects medication dosing.

N18.3 — CKD, stage 3 (most common in post-acute)N18.1 — Stage 1N18.2 — Stage 2N18.4 — Stage 4N18.5 — Stage 5N18.6 — End-stage renal disease (ESRD)
Medication / OrderNotes
Sevelamer (Renvela)Phosphate binder; stage 3–5
Sodium bicarbonateMetabolic acidosis correction
Calcitriol / Vitamin D analogsBone disease prevention in CKD
Dose adjustmentsMany medications require renal dose adjustment — flag for physician
CKD often coexists with hypertension and diabetes. Use combination codes I12.9 (hypertensive CKD) or E11.65 (DM with CKD) as directed by physician.

14. Osteoarthritis and arthritis

Osteoarthritis (OA) is wear-and-tear joint degeneration, most common in knees, hips, and hands. Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is autoimmune inflammatory joint disease. These are different conditions with different medications.

Very common in nursing home patients and often underdiagnosed. Depression in elderly patients may present as withdrawal, loss of appetite, or increased confusion rather than classic sadness. Document the diagnosis, not the symptom.

17. Dysphagia (swallowing difficulty)

Difficulty swallowing food or liquids. Very common in post-acute patients with stroke, dementia, or Parkinson's disease. Increases risk of aspiration pneumonia. Managed by speech therapy and dietary modifications.

R13.10 — Dysphagia, unspecified (most common)R13.11 — Oral phaseR13.12 — Oropharyngeal phaseR13.13 — Pharyngeal phaseR13.14 — Pharyngoesophageal phase
Order / InterventionPurpose

Thickened liquids (nectar or honey thick)Slow liquid flow to prevent aspiration
Aspiration precautionsUpright positioning during/after meals; small bites
Do NOT document "trouble swallowing" or "choking on food" — document dysphagia (R13.10) with the appropriate phase if known.

"Confusion," "altered mental status," "forgetfulness," and "agitation" are symptoms — not diagnoses. Always document the underlying diagnosis: dementia, cognitive impairment, or delirium as appropriate.

Mistake 3: Carrying forward resolved infection diagnoses

Remove once antibiotic course is complete and patient is clinically improved:

Pneumonia  ·  UTI  ·  Cellulitis / SSTI  ·  Wound infection (once healed)

Chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, AFib, dementia, COPD, hypothyroidism, CKD, osteoporosis) remain permanently on the active list.

Mistake 4: Overloading the diagnosis list

Each visit note should reflect only the top 3–4 diagnoses being actively managed at that encounter.

Mistake 5: Missing overnight nursing notes

Always review nursing notes before rounds. Flag falls, behavioral changes, new symptoms, abnormal vitals, or changes in mental status.
